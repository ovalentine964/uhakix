## SECTOR 1: NATIONAL INFRASTRUCTURE FUND (NIF) ACT, 2026

### 1.1 Current Status

**What happened:** On **March 9, 2026**, President William Ruto signed into law the **National Infrastructure Fund Bill (Act No. 4 of 2026)** after the National Assembly passed it on March 5–6, 2026. Sponsored by Majority Leader Kimani Ichung'wah, the law establishes a Limited Liability Company (LLC) — "the Fund" — that will mobilize and manage infrastructure financing outside the traditional parliamentary budget process.

**Key provisions of the law:**
- **Fund purpose:** Accelerate catalytic national infrastructure — national highways, railways, airports, seaports, electricity generation/transmission/distribution, water reservoirs, irrigation, agribusiness infrastructure
- **Mobilization target:** KES 5 trillion over the next 10 years
- **Funding sources:** Government allocations, private investment, privatization proceeds, grants, and loans
- **Governing Council:** Chaired by Treasury CS, includes CBK Governor, Attorney-General, and 6 presidential appointees (3-year terms) — provides strategic direction
- **Board of Directors:** 1 independent chairperson, Treasury CS, 4 independent directors, 2 development banking experienced directors, CEO as ex officio member
- **Investment Policy:** CS must submit to National Assembly for approval within 90 days
- **Penalties:** Misappropriation = repay 2× stolen amount, fine ≥ KES 10 million, or ≥ 5 years imprisonment
- **Audit:** Internal audit committee (NOT the Auditor General)

**What was sold to citizens:** Government claims this is a shift from debt-driven financing to an investment-led model, reducing reliance on public borrowing while accelerating development.

### 1.2 Why Citizens Are Concerned

**The central critique: "The government is auditing itself."**

1. **Constitutional bypass:** The fund is structured as an LLC. This means budgeting and approval are done by LLC shareholders, NOT by Parliament. Article 221 of the Constitution vests Parliament with authority to appropriate public funds — this law effectively strips away that safeguard.

2. **Internal audit replaces independent audit:** The law establishes an internal audit committee within the Fund instead of the mandatory constitutional audit by the Auditor General. This is the most concerning provision — **no independent oversight**.

3. **Treasury CS controls everything:** The same Cabinet Secretary is a board member, appoints directors, sets their remuneration, receives reports, AND determines staff appointments — effectively judge, jury, and executioner.

4. **No public participation:** Decisions are made in boardrooms. Citizens have no voice.

5. **Speed of passage:** Introduced as National Assembly Bill No. 1 of 2026, passed within weeks. Limited public debate on a law affecting 5 trillion shillings.

6. **Privatization proceeds:** The law explicitly allows proceeds from privatizing state-owned entities to flow into this fund — raising concerns about asset-stripping public institutions to feed a poorly overseen LLC.

### 1.5 Key Pain Points

- **Constitutional vulnerability:** The LLC structure bypasses Article 221 constitutional safeguards for public finance management
- **Audit capture:** The Fund audits itself via an internal committee — no independent verification
- **Centralized executive power:** Treasury CS has outsized control over governance, appointments, and reporting
- **Opaque investment decisions:** Private boardroom decisions, no public scrutiny
- **Asset-stripping risk:** Privatization proceeds of state-owned enterprises flow into an LLC with weakened oversight
- **Timeline:** The 90-day investment policy window — Parliament *can* reject, but this is the only check

1. **NIF Governance Tracker** — AI-powered monitoring of Board appointments (disqualification checks, conflict-of-interest flagging, political affiliation analysis)
2. **Constitutional Compliance Monitor** — Automated analysis of whether NIF operations comply with Article 221; alert when bypasses occur

4. **Privatization-Audit Cross-Reference** — Track all state asset sales and follow the money — where did proceeds go?
5. **NIF Dashboard** — Simple citizen-facing page showing: how much has been mobilized, what projects funded, who the board members are, what audits exist
6. **Public Participation Channel** — Enable citizens to submit questions/concerns about specific NIF projects or contracts

## SECTOR 2: SOCIAL HEALTH AUTHORITY (SHA)

### 2.1 Current Status

SHA was launched in **October 2024** to replace the scandal-ridden National Health Insurance Fund (NHIF), under the Social Health Insurance Act of 2023. It was marketed as a fraud-proof digital revolution that would guarantee every Kenyan healthcare without financial hardship.

**Reality check:** Less than 18 months later, SHA is at the center of a **multi-billion shilling corruption scandal** involving ghost facilities, unpaid hospital claims, inflated contracts, and fake patient billing.

**Key facts:**
- SHA is a **KES 104 billion system**
- Health Cabinet Secretary Aden Duale has been called to resign by Kenyans and activists
- Current SHA CEO: Dr Mercy Mwangangi (replaced Robert Ingasira who was arrested)
- 10,000 out of 17,755 active health facilities registered under SHA
- 40+ facilities suspended for fraud across 12 counties
- 9 people arrested (including ex-CEO) in October 2025 for financial irregularities
- **NO CONVCTIONS** have been made on individuals charged in the scandal
- SHA pulled down its online hospital payment list and health facility registry (covering tracks)
- CS Duale claims advanced analytics and AI in SHA detect fraud in real time — yet fraud persisted

### 2.2 Estimated Annual Loss

| Figure | Description | Source |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| **KES 104 billion** | Total value of SHA's digital system contract — awarded without competitive bidding, no clear scope, ownership with contractor | Auditor General, March 2025 |
| **KES 11 billion** | Lost to fraud between Oct 2024 – Apr 2025 (first 6 months of operation) | Ministry of Health audit, reported Jan 2026 |
| **KES 10.6 billion** | Fraudulent claims flagged from rogue hospitals | Daily Nation, Aug 2025 |
| **KES 5 billion** | Lost through ghost/non-existent hospitals claiming insurance | Wikipedia, Ministry of Health |
| **KES 75 billion** | Unpaid claims to legitimate hospitals (as of Aug 2025) | Whispers.co.ke investigation |
| **KES 17.5 million** | Defrauded by ex-CEO Robert Ingasira and 8 others (arrested Oct 2025) | The Standard, Oct 2025 |

**Total confirmed fraud/losses: KES 26+ billion in first year of operation** (excluding the disputed KES 104B IT procurement contract). Given the scale of unpaid legitimate claims (KES 75B), the actual healthcare value delivered vs. money collected is deeply negative.

### 2.3 Revenue Collected vs. Actual Healthcare Spending

- **Revenue collected:** SHA collects premiums through monthly payroll deductions (2.5% of gross salary for formal employees, minimum KES 300 for informal sector). Annual payment option was later demanded.
- **Healthcare spending:** Legitimate hospitals are owed **KES 75 billion** in unpaid claims, meaning far less than collected is actually reaching patients.
- **Gap:** The money collected significantly exceeds what reaches actual healthcare providers. Billions vanish through: ghost claims, inflated IT contracts, unauthorized deductions (2.5% and 5% deductions unexplained), and money routed through secret escrow accounts.
- SHA scrapped monthly premiums and demanded full annual payments upfront — locking out low-income Kenyans.

### 2.5 Key Pain Points

1. **Ghost hospitals exist on paper and get paid:** Facilities that don't physically exist received millions — a closed maternity hospital got KES 5M, a non-existent Homa Bay clinic got KES 2.8M, a nursing home registered one day before payout got KES 3.8M
2. **Fraud types:** Ghost patients, double billing, pre-authorization code abuse, outpatient-to-inpatient conversion, fake ICU/HDU claims, blood donation patients billed as 5-day admissions
3. **Genuine hospitals owed billions:** SHA hasn't paid legitimate hospital claims (KES 75B+), forcing hospitals to stop accepting SHA patients
4. **Patient burden:** Families forced to pay cash, borrow money; SHA demands full annual payments excluding the poor
5. **Cover-up behavior:** SHA pulled down online payment lists and facility registry to hide evidence
6. **No convictions:** 9 people arrested, 40+ facilities suspended, 12 doctors blacklisted — but ZERO convictions
7. **SHA doesn't control its own IT system:** Managed by Ministry of Health and Digital Health Authority — SHA is vulnerable with no oversight
8. **Political pressure:** Saboti MP Caleb Amisi called it a "national security issue"

6. **SHA Scandal Timeline & Archive** — Permanent record of SHA fraud cases, even when government deletes data
7. **Citizen SHA Reporting Tool** — Report instances of being turned away at hospitals, overcharges, or suspicious facility registrations
8. **AI Fraud Pattern Detector** — Analyze public SHA payment data for suspicious patterns (ghost claims, unusual billing spikes, duplicate payments)

## SECTOR 3: E-CITIZEN PLATFORM

### 3.1 Current Status

e-Citizen is Kenya's flagship digital platform that centralizes access to **over 22,000 government services** since 2023 — from passports and ID applications to business registration and land services. Every Kenyan accessing government services online must go through this platform.

**CRITICAL: The Auditor General and Parliament's Public Accounts Committee (PAC) have called e-Citizen a "crime scene" with no legal framework, demanding its immediate shutdown.**

**Key revelations:**
- **No statutory framework:** e-Citizen operates without any legal foundation — no law validates or regulates it
- **PAC statement:** "Let's be honest... we should not be using this system. It has no legal backing whatsoever" — PAC Chair Tindi Mwale
- **Multiple ministries involved** (Treasury, ICT, Interior) with no clear accountability
- **Private company control:** Originally developed by World Bank IFC, operated by **Webmasters Kenya Ltd** — a private entity
- **Auditor General was denied access** to the platform for data analysis

### 3.2 Estimated Annual Loss / Financial Irregularities

| Figure | Description | Source |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| **KES 11 billion** | Irregular transactions diverted to private accounts | Auditor General Nancy Gathungu, 2025 |
| **KES 44.8 billion** | Revenue collected in 2023/24 that remains unaccounted for | Auditor General report |
| **KES 6.3 billion** | Diverted to unauthorized "Pesaflow" account (KES 68.7M + USD 48M) | Auditor General, confirmed by PAC March 2026 |
| **KES 127.8 million** | Unauthorized out-of-court settlement to Goldrock Ltd | Auditor General |
| **KES 492.2 million** | Paid to "Electronic Citizen Solutions Ltd" — not party to the official contract | Auditor General |
| **KES 2.2 billion** | Ilregular convenience fees charged to Kenyans | Daily Nation |
| **KES 10 billion** | In collection/settlement accounts in limbo, no legally binding agreements | Tuko, Auditor General |
| **KES 515 million** | Tourism Fund variance (due: KES 2.24B, collected: KES 1.72B) | Auditor General |

**Total confirmed irregularities: KES 68+ billion** in discrepancies across the platform's history.

**Revenue scale:** e-Citizen collects money from 22,000+ services used by millions of Kenyans. The platform collects **billions monthly** — but where it goes, no one can track.

### 3.4 Key Pain Points

1. **KES 50 convenience fee per transaction** — charged on EVERY transaction regardless of value. A KES 20 product can incur KES 50 fee (250% markup). This fee lacks legal backing and makes public services disproportionately expensive.

2. **Healthcare victims:** Convenience fees charged at every service point during a single hospital visit — disproportionately affects low-income patients, directly undermining universal healthcare.

3. **"Pesaflow" mystery account:** KES 6.3 billion appeared from an unidentified account "Pesaflow" — not listed in National Treasury's approved collection accounts. The Auditor General couldn't get bank statements.

4. **No legal framework:** The entire platform operates in legal limbo — no law, no oversight body, no regulatory structure.

5. **Manual reconciliation:** The Auditor General found that reconciliation between e-Citizen reports and Treasury funds was done MANUALLY — prone to errors, hiding discrepancies.

6. **Help desk absence:** No incident resolution mechanism — citizens' problems go unresolved indefinitely.

7. **Data security risk:** A centralized platform holding all Kenyan citizens' data with weak legal protections — cybersecurity experts frame this as a national security crisis.

8. **Government doesn't own source code:** The platform's IP may still belong to private contractors.

### 3.5 Recommended UUHAKIX Features

1. **e-Citizen Revenue Tracker** — Aggregate and display all publicly reported e-Citizen revenue data with irregularity flags
2. **Convenience Fee Calculator** — Show citizens the true cost of government services including hidden convenience fees
3. **Legal Status Monitor** — Track legislative developments on whether/when a legal framework for e-Citizen will be enacted
4. **Audit Findings Archive** — Permanent archive of all Auditor General findings on e-Citizen (protected from government deletion)
5. **e-Citizen Service Status Dashboard** — Which services are working, which have issues, what fees apply
6. **Citizen Complaint Aggregator** — Collect and aggregate citizen reports about e-Citizen failures, overcharging, service denials
7. **Data Protection Watch** — Monitor e-Citizen's compliance with Kenya Data Protection Act 2019

## SECTOR 4: COUNTY GOVERNMENT SPENDING

### 4.1 Current Status

Kenya's 47 county governments collectively receive approximately **KES 300–360 billion annually** in equitable share from the national government plus own-source revenue. The Controller of Budget (COB) is constitutionally mandated to authorize all county withdrawals from public funds.

**The Auditor General's 2023/24 report exposed "massive corruption" and "widespread malpractices" across almost all county governments.**

**Key findings from AG report:**

**Mombasa County:**
- Discrepancies in financial statements
- Unexplained voided transactions
- Retention money (KES 11.6M and KES 22M) misclassified
- Street lighting (KES 13.7M) misclassified as office furniture
- Non-compliance with wage bill regulations
- Irregular staff promotions

**Kwale County:**
- Irregular recruitment (116 employees, ZERO persons with disability — violating 5% quota)
- KES 4M irregularly paid to Council of Governors for hospitality at Devolution Conference

**Kilifi County:**
- Stalled projects, unsupported emergency relief payments
- Retention of employees beyond retirement age

**Kiambu County:**
- Direct procurement of 150,000 chicks for KES 45M — no contract number, no PPRA reporting
- Grafted mango/pawpaw/orange seedlings and grass cuttings for KES 6.12M via direct procurement

**Murang'a County:**
- KES 66.2 million on legal cases with NO established legal department — no value-for-money verification

**Nairobi County:**
- Dishi na County school feeding programme: County contracted Food for Education at KES 25/plate, learners paid KES 5, county PAID KES 30/plate — exceeding contracted amount (KES 5/plate overcharging)

- EACC has investigated NG-CDF irregularities in multiple constituencies
- School bursary funds often distributed along political lines rather than merit/need

## ADDITIONAL CRITICAL SECTORS TO MONITOR

### 6.1 State-Owned Enterprises (SOE) Privatization

**Why monitor:** The NIF Act explicitly channels privatization proceeds into the Fund. Kenya is actively selling shares in state-owned entities (KPLC, Kenya Airways, etc.). **Where is the money going?** No transparent tracking exists.

**Key risks:** Assets sold at undervalued prices, proceeds diverted, no public accounting.

**Recommended feature:** SOE Privatization Tracker — Monitor all state asset sales, prices, buyers, and destination of proceeds.

### 6.2 Public Debt Management

**Why monitor:** While NIF aims to reduce borrowing, Kenya's public debt stands at **KES 13+ trillion**. Debt servicing consumes a disproportionate share of revenue.

**Key risks:** Off-budget borrowing through NIF LLC, currency mismatch risks, non-concessional loans.

**Recommended feature:** Debt Watch — Track all Kenyan government debt (on and off-balance-sheet), debt-to-GDP ratios, servicing costs, and creditor profiles.

### 6.3 Climate Finance & Carbon Credits

**Why monitor:** Kenya is positioning itself as an African climate finance leader, with carbon credit frameworks and green bond markets. These are **new, untested financial instruments** with minimal oversight.

**Key risks:** Carbon credit revenue capture by elites, fake offset projects, international fund mismanagement.

**Recommended feature:** Climate Finance Monitor — Track climate finance inflows, carbon credit deals, and environmental project delivery.

### 6.4 Judiciary & Justice System

**Why monitor:** The EACC forwarded 44 files to the DPP (Jan-Mar 2026), but only 4 were approved for prosecution. The justice system is the bottleneck preventing accountability.

**Key risks:** Case dismissal, delayed prosecutions, political interference.

**Recommended feature:** Justice Tracker — Monitor corruption cases from investigation to final judgment, flag dismissals, delays, and unusual outcomes.

### 6.5 Political Party Funding & Campaign Finance

**Why monitor:** Campaign spending vastly exceeds reported limits. Unknown funders influence policy. This is the root of political capture.

**Key risks:** Illicit funding, policy capture, quid pro quo arrangements.

**Recommended feature:** Campaign Finance Watch — Track political party income sources, expenditure reports, and potential conflicts of interest.

## KEY INSIGHTS FOR UUHAKIX STRATEGY

1. **SHA is the most urgent sector** — A KES 104 billion system hemorrhaging KES 26+ billion in confirmed fraud while citizens suffer. This is UUHAKIX's most compelling entry point for public attention.
